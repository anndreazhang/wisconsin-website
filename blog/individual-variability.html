Individual Variability in Sevoflurane Sensitivity

By Sally Li

Sevoflurane is a widely-used anesthetic agent valued for its rapid uptake and emergence characteristics. However, despite predictable population-level pharmacokinetics, individual patients exhibit considerable differences in their response to this volatile anesthetic.

Age and MAC

Age stands as one of the most reproducible determinants of sevoflurane requirement. The minimum alveolar concentration (MAC) represents the standard population measurement for volatile anesthetic potency. In middle-aged adults, sevoflurane's MAC typically measures around 2%, though this figure varies considerably.

Researchers have developed age-related MAC charts demonstrating that anesthetic concentration needs decline progressively with advancing age. This distinction matters clinically — failing to adjust dosing appropriately risks hypotension or delayed emergence in elderly patients, while relying exclusively on adult MAC values may provide insufficient dosing for younger populations.

Pharmacokinetic Profile

Sevoflurane's favorable pharmacokinetic profile stems from its low blood–gas partition coefficient, which supports rapid equilibration. Research by Behne and colleagues established that sevoflurane uptake and elimination depend largely on solubility, ventilation, circulation, and tissue distribution, with minimal metabolic conversion. Even in special populations — obese patients, children, and those with renal insufficiency — responses remain predictable at the group level, though metabolic byproducts like serum fluoride may differ.

Intraindividual Variation

Lambert's investigation of 30 adults receiving standardized 6% sevoflurane induction revealed striking variability: loss of consciousness ranged from 45 to 135 seconds, while time to minimum BIS varied from 120 to 720 seconds. Notably, demographic factors including age, weight, and baseline heart rate failed to predict response timing reliably.

Psychological and Nociceptive Factors

Psychological and nociceptive factors also influence maintenance anesthetic requirements. Kil's research found that preoperative anxiety predicted propofol requirements during induction, while pain sensitivity correlated with sevoflurane MAC-hours needed to maintain target BIS levels. This suggests that anesthetic depth involves hypnosis, analgesia, autonomic responsiveness, and immobility — each varying individually — and that maintenance concentrations may reflect individual nociceptive processing rather than purely hypnotic needs.

Clinical Implications

Age-adjusted MAC provides an essential starting point, but it is not a substitute for individualized titration.

Clinicians should integrate end-tidal concentration, BIS trends, hemodynamic responses, and patient-specific characteristics into their dosing decisions. A rigid, one-size-fits-all approach to sevoflurane dosing overlooks the meaningful biological variation that exists across patient populations and even within ostensibly similar demographic groups.
