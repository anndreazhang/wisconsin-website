Tools to Measure Intraoperative Nociception and Analgesia

By Sally Li

Traditional reliance on hemodynamic indicators like heart rate and blood pressure proves inadequate for assessing intraoperative pain management. These metrics can be influenced by anesthesia depth, vasoactive medications, and patient physiology rather than actual pain levels, making it difficult for clinicians to distinguish true nociceptive responses from other physiological events.

The Limitations of Hemodynamic Monitoring

For decades, perioperative analgesia management has depended on hemodynamic parameters — heart rate, blood pressure, and respiratory rate — as indirect surrogates for nociception. However, these responses are nonspecific and can be blunted or exaggerated by anesthetic agents, beta-blockers, vasopressors, and baseline cardiovascular conditions. The result is that clinicians frequently face uncertainty: is a rise in blood pressure a sign of inadequate analgesia, or a pharmacological effect of another drug?

This ambiguity has driven the development of dedicated nociception monitoring technologies that target more specific physiological pathways.

Key Monitoring Tools

Surgical Pleth Index (SPI)

The SPI calculates a score based on normalized heartbeat intervals and photoplethysmographic pulse wave amplitudes, providing a real-time estimate of the balance between nociception and analgesia. While clinically useful, accuracy may be compromised by changes in vascular tone, arrhythmias, and catecholamines.

Pupillary Dilation Reflex (PDR)

Pupillometry monitors the pupillary response to standardized painful stimuli. Because pupillary dilation is largely mediated by the sympathetic nervous system and remains relatively independent of sympathetic activation from non-nociceptive sources, it can offer a more specific signal. However, high-dose opioids can suppress the reflex, reducing sensitivity.

Analgesia Nociception Index (ANI)

The ANI utilizes heart rate variability to assess parasympathetic function as a proxy for nociceptive state. A higher ANI score indicates greater parasympathetic tone and is associated with adequate analgesia, while lower scores suggest a nociceptive response.

Nociception Level (NOL) Index

The NOL integrates multiple physiological signals — including skin conductance, temperature, photoplethysmographic amplitude, and pulse wave interval — into a single composite index. This multimodal approach is designed to capture the complex, multidimensional nature of the nociceptive response more comprehensively than any single parameter.

Clinical Evidence

Research indicates these monitors can significantly reduce intraoperative opioid consumption. A comparison of pupillometry versus SPI-guided analgesia found that patients in the pupillometry group required significantly less intraoperative remifentanil and reported lower peak postoperative pain scores, with shorter extubation times and reduced postoperative nausea.

However, concerns exist regarding NOL-guided therapy potentially increasing surgical stress responses in certain contexts, and the need for age-specific reference ranges has been identified as an area requiring further validation.

Looking Ahead

The future of intraoperative analgesia monitoring likely lies in combining multiple modalities and incorporating machine learning algorithms to distinguish nociceptive responses from confounding factors. As these technologies mature, individualized analgesia titration — analogous to the shift from population-level to patient-specific dosing — may become standard clinical practice.
